The diagnosis of food allergies was the subject of comparative research conducted in 2013 by Philippe Bégin and Kari C. Nadeau. Research has shown that it may be difficult to interpret allergy test findings and that the patient history is often incorrect when screening for and treating food allergies [7].... Consequently, relying exclusively on medical history to monitor dietary allergens that cause allergies will not be adequate. Additionally, studies have shown that when an allergen enters the stomach, in addition to instantly causing local symptoms (skin responses) upon contact with the meal, it may also result in excruciating stomach pain and prolonged vomiting. Therefore, it is conceivable to infer that methods for recording symptoms may be used to identify potential food allergies. To determine the links between food consumption, mental discomfort, and digestive issues, Egbert Clevers, Hans Törnblom, Magnus Simrén, Jan Tack, and Lukas Van Oudenhove performed another study in 2016.
The major goal of this study was to demonstrate how patterns in food consumption and symptomatology may be utilized to anticipate digestive issues. A variety of challenges relating to the forecasting and assessment of the input data utilizing tools like machine learning have also been addressed in the study. It has been shown that there is a link between food consumption and symptoms, which may be examined using deep learning and machine learning. Find Me Gluten Free an application used to suggest the Gluten Free Restaurants. Finding restaurants that can accommodate your needs can be made easier with Find Me Gluten Free if you have celiac disease, wheat sensitivity, or you simply need to avoid gluten [7]. This application only focuses on the Gluten allergen. Wheat (wheatberries, durum, emmer, semolina, spelt, farina, farro, graham, KAMUT® Khorasan wheat, and einkorn), rye, barley, and triticale, a hybrid between wheat and rye, all contain the protein known as gluten. By serving as a glue to hold food components together, gluten aids in the preservation of food form. Even items you would not expect to contain gluten can be discovered in copious quantities [14]. Although Celiac Disease exists, there is no such thing as a gluten allergy. Undiagnosed or untreated celiac disease is a digestive issue that might become dangerous. A rash, extreme weight loss or failure to appropriately gain weight, severe diarrhea after consuming gluten-containing foods, and stomach discomfort are all signs of celiac disease. You could only notice poor weight gain and no discomfort or other symptoms in young children. The only person qualified to diagnose celiac disease is a gastroenterologist with board certification. Because avoiding gluten is the main form of therapy, it must also be done when the patient consumes gluten-containing foods. There are presently no diagnostic tests for an appropriate diagnosis of a gluten intolerance, which is not an allergy. Although only a small percentage of persons with gluten intolerance have celiac disease, those who exhibit particular symptoms may need to be tested. Gluten intolerance is not a sign that you should get tested for allergies and is not a disease for which an allergist can treat. Unfortunately, many people who claim to be "sensitive" to gluten restrict their diet without first seeing a doctor. If there is a worry about celiac disease, people with gluten sensitivity should visit their health care physician or be referred to a gastroenterologist. Diabetes, one of the most prevalent non-communicable illnesses in modern society, was the focus of Pivithuru and Hewa Bajjagamage Beenuka's study. Therefore, the goal of this study was to use technology to improve the lives of diabetes patients by providing customized meal plans that are updated often. A mobile Android application will be used as the proposed solution to provide different meal options. The recommended method enables users to see their diabetes status, that is, whether they have diabetes or not, in addition to offering meal planning. Based on patient blood report records and BMI values, a manually constructed dataset was used for the method of creating meal plans, and secondary data were used for the purpose of predicting diabetes status. Techniques from machine learning were used in this performance. Three classification models were trained independently to determine the diabetes status and offer meal plans for the user. With respect to the three models developed, Random Forest had the highest accuracy rates for meal plan suggestions and the challenge of predicting diabetes status, both at 72.9%.
